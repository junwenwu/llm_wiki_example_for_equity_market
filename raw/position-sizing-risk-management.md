## What Is Position Sizing?

Position sizing refers to the number of units invested in a particular security by an investor or trader. It is an important part of an investment strategy because it impacts both risk management and potential returns.

An investor's account size and risk tolerance should be taken into account when determining position size, making it especially relevant for day traders, forex traders, and others looking to balance risk and reward effectively.

### Key Takeaways

- Position sizing helps investors determine how many units of a security to purchase to control risk and maximize returns.
- Investors should consider their account size and risk tolerance when determining appropriate position sizing.
- Proper position sizing is crucial for day traders and forex traders due to the fast-moving nature of these markets.
- Investors risk losing more than intended if a stock gap occurs below their stop-loss order.
- Reducing position size can help mitigate gap risk during high volatility periods, such as earnings announcements.

## The Basics of Position Sizing in Investing

Position sizing refers to the size of a position within a particular portfolio, or the dollar amount that an investor is going to trade. Investors use position sizing to help determine how many units of security they can purchase, which helps them to control risk and maximize returns.

Position sizing is crucial in many investment types, but it's most often linked with day trading and forex.

## Practical Example of Position Sizing

Using correct position sizing involves weighing three different factors:

### 1. Account Risk

Before using correct position sizing for a trade, investors must first figure out their account risk. This typically gets expressed as a percentage of the investor's capital.

**Rule of thumb: Most retail investors risk no more than 2% of their investment capital on any one trade; fund managers usually risk less than this amount.**

**Example:**
- If an investor has a $25,000 account and decides to set their maximum account risk at 2%, they cannot risk more than $500 per trade (2% × $25,000).
- Even if the investor loses 10 consecutive trades in a row, they have only lost 20% of their investment capital.

### 2. Trade Risk

The investor must then determine where to place their stop-loss order for the specific trade. For stock trades, the trade risk is the dollar difference between the entry price and the stop-loss.

**Example:**
- If an investor intends to purchase Apple Inc. at $160 and place a stop-loss order at $140, the trade risk is $20 per share.

### 3. Proper Position Size

The investor now knows that they can risk $500 per trade and is risking $20 per share.

**Formula: Position Size = Account Risk ÷ Trade Risk**

To work out the correct position size:
- Account risk: $500
- Trade risk: $20 per share
- Position size: $500 ÷ $20 = **25 shares**

## Managing Gap Risk With Position Sizing

Investors should be aware that even if they use correct position sizing, they may lose more than their specified account risk limit if a stock **gaps** below their stop-loss order.

**Mitigation strategy:** If expecting volatility, like before earnings reports, investors might halve their position size to cut gap risk.

## The 1% and 2% Rules

### The 1% Rule
Never risk more than 1% of your total account on a single trade.
- More conservative
- Suitable for beginners or smaller accounts
- Allows for longer losing streaks without significant capital damage

### The 2% Rule
Never risk more than 2% of your total account on a single trade.
- Standard for most retail traders
- Balance between growth potential and capital preservation

## Position Sizing Formula

```
Position Size = (Account Size × Risk Percentage) ÷ (Entry Price - Stop Loss Price)
```

### Example Calculation:

**Given:**
- Account size: $10,000
- Risk per trade: 2% ($200)
- Entry price: $50
- Stop loss: $48

**Calculation:**
- Risk per share: $50 - $48 = $2
- Position size: $200 ÷ $2 = 100 shares
- Total position value: 100 × $50 = $5,000

## Why Position Sizing Matters

1. **Capital Preservation**: Protects your trading capital from large drawdowns
2. **Emotional Control**: Pre-calculated position sizes remove emotion from trading decisions
3. **Consistency**: Enables systematic approach to risk management
4. **Longevity**: Keeps you in the game long enough to benefit from your edge
5. **Compounding**: Protects gains and allows for sustainable growth

## The Bottom Line

Position sizing is essential for managing risk and optimizing returns by aligning trade size with account size and risk tolerance. Investors should factor in account and trade risk to set effective stop-loss orders and avoid heavy losses, while staying alert to gap risk during volatile periods. Regularly reassessing position sizing ensures it remains aligned with evolving goals and market conditions.
